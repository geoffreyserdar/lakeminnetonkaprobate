What probate really is

A bridge between the life that was and the legacy that continues.

Most people hear the word probate and feel their stomach tighten. But probate isn't here to make life harder — it's built to protect families. It simply means the court is supervising how an estate is transferred after someone passes, making sure debts are paid and heirs receive what was intended.

"My work begins where the paperwork meets real life. Attorneys handle the legal and intellectual dimension of probate. I coordinate the material and logistical responsibilities — the work that unfolds inside the home and on the ground."

When both sides work together, families feel supported from every angle. That structure and compassion can exist together.

A real-world example

What probate looks like, start to finish.

Let's imagine a woman named Mary. Mary lived in Chanhassen, owned a home in Victoria, and had accounts in Minnetonka. When Mary passes, her son David and daughter Clare are left to figure out what comes next.

Court appoints a personal representativeDavid meets with a probate attorney who handles the filings. Once approved, David receives Letters — proof he's authorized to make decisions for the estate.
Inventory what Mary ownedThe attorney and David account for everything: the home, accounts, investments, vehicle. Some assets — retirement accounts, life insurance with named beneficiaries — skip probate entirely.
Managing the home while the law movesThe house still needs care. Mail keeps coming. This is where I come in — walking the property, helping the family decide what to keep, and preparing the home respectfully. Start with respect. It isn't just a property. It's someone's story.
Prepare, list, and closeWith the attorney's guidance, we prepare the home thoughtfully — not with big remodels, but with clean, simple improvements. Offers are reviewed with both the family and the attorney. Proceeds go to the estate for distribution.

Common pitfalls

Most of the pain comes from avoidable mistakes.

Families are grieving and making decisions under pressure. Here's what I see most often — and how to avoid it.

01

Waiting to act

Out of respect or uncertainty, families pause for months. Probate already moves slowly. Waiting adds time and cost without adding peace.

02

Trying to do it alone

Probate involves legal filings, property care, and emotion. Attorneys and realtors exist to share that weight — keeping everything lawful and calm.

03

Fire-selling the home

Rushing rarely brings peace. Preparation and proper marketing protect the estate's legacy and the family's outcome.

04

No neutral voice in family conflict

Emotions run high and disagreements are normal. My role — alongside the attorney — is to keep everyone focused on the estate's intentions, not the pressure of the moment.
